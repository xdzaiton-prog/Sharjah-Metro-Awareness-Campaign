About the Issue

Sharjah residents who rely on the Dubai Metro face two major challenges: overcrowding on Dubai Metro peak services (especially the Red Line), and the absence of a metro network inside Sharjah, which forces many residents to travel by bus or car to reach the Dubai system. This campaign collects evidence, personal stories, and policy recommendations to present to the RTA and the Sharjah Executive Council.

Formal Proposal Letter

To: Roads and Transport Authority (RTA Dubai) & Sharjah Executive Council

Subject: Urgent Proposal to Address Metro Overcrowding and the Need for a Sharjah Metro Link

Respected Authorities,

I am writing on behalf of daily commuters residing in Sharjah who rely on the Dubai Metro system for essential transportation to schools, universities, and workplaces. While the Dubai Metro has been globally praised for its efficiency, the current peak-hour congestion has reached critical levels — with trains becoming so overcrowded that many commuters are unable to board despite multiple train arrivals. This issue not only creates delays and physical exhaustion but also raises serious safety concerns, particularly on the Red Line between Union and BurJuman stations.

Furthermore, Sharjah currently does not have a metro system of its own, resulting in tens of thousands of residents relying on buses or private vehicles just to reach the nearest Dubai Metro station. This increases road congestion, carbon emissions, and pressure on existing infrastructure. A strategic collaborative effort between Sharjah and Dubai — including the introduction of a dedicated Sharjah Metro line or direct metro extension — would provide long-term, sustainable relief.

I kindly urge both authorities to consider immediate peak-hour frequency optimization for the Dubai Metro, and to initiate a joint Sharjah–Dubai feasibility study for metro integration. This solution aligns directly with the UAE’s Vision 2030 goals of sustainability, urban innovation, and citizen wellbeing.

Thank you for your leadership and continued commitment to improving public transportation.

Sincerely,
Zaid Mtanious
Grade 12 Student, ADIS Sharjah

Persuasive Op‑Ed

Use this op-ed for school newspapers, local blogs, or to pitch to regional publications.

Every morning, thousands of Sharjah residents set their alarms a little earlier — not to catch an early workout or savor breakfast, but simply to survive the morning commute. They pour into cars, buses, and shared rides, inching toward the Dubai Metro stations, only to find themselves waiting outside packed trains, pressed shoulder to shoulder with other exhausted commuters. This daily struggle is not just an inconvenience; it’s a silent crisis in our urban planning.

Sharjah and Dubai are twin cities — interconnected by purpose, economy, and community — yet divided by transportation. The absence of a metro system in Sharjah, combined with the severe overcrowding on the Dubai Metro’s Red Line, has made daily commuting a test of endurance. Students arrive at school drained before classes begin. Employees arrive late despite leaving home early. Families spend more time in traffic than they do together at dinner. This is not the vision of progress that the UAE strives for.

Public transport defines the rhythm of a city’s life. Expanding the metro network into Sharjah would not only reduce road congestion and carbon emissions but also enhance productivity and wellbeing. According to the RTA’s 2023–24 figures, more than 230 million riders used the Dubai Metro last year — a substantial increase from previous years — placing immense strain on the Red Line. Imagine fewer cars clogging Al Ittihad Road, cleaner air, and quieter mornings. A Sharjah Metro link would empower thousands to move freely, sustainably, and safely — all while supporting the UAE’s Vision 2030 goals.

Of course, infrastructure projects require time, funding, and coordination. Yet the cost of inaction is far greater: lost hours, heightened stress, and diminished quality of life for tens of thousands. The solution is not just to increase train frequency during peak hours — though that would be a welcome immediate fix — but to envision a regional network that connects both emirates as seamlessly as their economies already are.

Sharjah and Dubai have repeatedly proven that collaboration can turn challenges into milestones. It’s time to extend that spirit underground — in steel, rail, and shared vision. A metro link between the two cities is not merely a transportation upgrade; it’s an investment in people, progress, and the promise of a better commute for all.
